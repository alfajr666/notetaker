To establish a newly founded crypto exchange (legally referred to as a **Pedagang Aset Keuangan Digital** or "Trader") and obtain full approval from the **OJK** (Financial Services Authority) under **POJK No. 27 of 2024**, an entity must fulfill rigorous institutional, financial, and operational requirements.

### **Key Requirements for Pedagang Approval**

- **Legal Entity:** Must be a Indonesian **Perseroan Terbatas (PT)**.
- **Minimum Capital:** A minimum paid-up capital of **Rp 100 billion** (approx. $6.2 million) is required.
- **Maintained Equity:** The entity must maintain a minimum equity of **Rp 50 billion** at all times.
- **Source of Funds:** Capital must not originate from loans or illicit activities such as money laundering or terrorism financing.
- **Fit and Proper Test:** All "Primary Parties," including directors, commissioners, and controlling shareholders, must pass OJK’s **fit-and-proper test** to ensure integrity, competence, and financial reputation.
- **Organizational Structure:** Must include dedicated divisions for Information Technology, Audit, Legal, Consumer Complaints, Client Support, and Accounting/Finance.
- **IT Security:** At least one employee must hold a **CISSP** (Certified Information Systems Security Professional) certification.
- **Consumer Onboarding:** Must use **Regulatory Technology (RegTech)** for onboarding, including biometric verification and face recognition with liveness detection, integrated with national population data.

### **Metrics for Continuous Monitoring**

Regulated exchanges must monitor specific financial and operational ratios to avoid breaching OJK regulations:

1. **Equity Threshold (The Crypto "MKBD" Equivalent):** Unlike traditional security firms that monitor **MKBD** (Adjusted Net Working Capital), crypto traders must monitor their **Maintained Equity**. They are strictly required to keep equity at a minimum of **Rp 50 billion**. For the **Bursa** (the central exchange entity), this threshold is higher, requiring equity to be at least **80% of its Rp 1 trillion** paid-up capital.
    
2. **Asset Storage Ratios (30/70 Rule):**
    
    - **External Storage:** A Trader can only store a maximum of **30%** of the total consumer-owned crypto assets themselves; the remaining **70%** must be stored with a licensed **Custodian** (Pengelola Tempat Penyimpanan).
    - **Internal Segregation:** Of the 30% stored internally, at least **70%** must be kept in **cold storage** (offline) to mitigate cyber risks, while a maximum of **30%** may be in **hot storage** (online) for liquidity.
3. **100% Fund Segregation:** Traders must ensure that **100% of consumer funds** are placed in separate accounts at the **Clearing and Settlement Institution** (Lembaga Kliring Penjaminan dan Penyelesaian). They are strictly prohibited from using these funds for their own operations.
    
4. **Transaction and Reporting:** Traders must maintain **real-time recording** of all transactions. They are required to provide **read-only access** to their systems for OJK supervision and submit daily, monthly, quarterly, and annual reports.
    

### **Comparison with Traditional Finance (MKBD)**

In traditional finance, security and asset management firms use **MKBD** to ensure they have enough liquid assets to cover their risks. In the Indonesian crypto sector, OJK has simplified this into a **fixed minimum equity requirement** (Rp 50 billion for traders) combined with strict **asset segregation and storage ratios**. While both frameworks aim to ensure solvency and protect consumer assets, the crypto framework places a heavier emphasis on the **physical/digital location of the assets** (Cold vs. Hot storage and Custodian vs. Trader) due to the high cybersecurity risks inherent in digital assets.
