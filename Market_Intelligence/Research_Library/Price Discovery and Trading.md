This article by **Kevin Mak** explains the fundamental concept of **price discovery**—the process by which markets aggregate dispersed information into a single price. Using a classroom trading simulation as a framework, Mak illustrates how even when individual traders have only partial information, the market as a whole can converge to the “correct” price through trading activity.

## 🧠 **Core Concepts Explained**
### 1. **What is Price Discovery?**
- Markets aggregate **perfect but incomplete information** from all participants.
- No single trader knows everything, but **collectively, the market knows**.
- Through trading, participants reveal their private information, leading to an **efficient price** that reflects all available knowledge.

### 2. **The Classroom Simulation: A Takeover Scenario**
- **Scenario:** A company will be acquired by one of five bidders with offers: $20, $22, $24, $26, $28.
- **Mechanics:**
    - Each trader receives **private, correct, but incomplete information** (e.g., “Bidder A drops out”).
    - Over time, each trader learns three pieces of information, narrowing possible outcomes.
    - **Example:** One trader might know the final price is either $22 or $28; another knows it’s either $20 or $22.
- **Result:** The market price converges to the true takeover price before the simulation ends, even though no individual knows the final answer.

### 3. **Key Learning: Market vs. Individual Perspective**
- **Individual perspective:** “What do I think it’s worth?” (based on private info)
- **Market perspective:** “What is the market telling me?” (based on aggregated info)
- **Critical insight:** A trader’s edge comes not from having better information alone, but from **interpreting how their information fits with market behavior**.

### 4. **Why Many Traders Lose in the Simulation**
- They rely on **expected value calculations** without considering **others’ information**.
- **Example:** A trader knowing ($22, $28) sees the price at $23 and buys, thinking it’s undervalued. But another trader knowing ($20, $22) sees the same $23 price as overvalued and sells. The seller has better-aligned information with the final outcome.

### 5. **Real-World Application: Thinking About Counterparties**
- Successful traders ask: **“Who is on the other side of my trade, and what do they know?”**
- **Market participants can be categorized:**
    - **Informed:** Insiders, fundamental analysts, quants
    - **Uninformed:** Index funds (rebalancing), retail traders (social media-driven), gamblers
- Understanding **flow motivation** helps gauge confidence in your own thesis.

### 6. **The Limits of Public Information**
- A thesis like “P/E is low, so it’s cheap” is **not an edge**—it’s public knowledge already priced in.
- **Real alpha requires:** Information the market **doesn’t have**, or a **better interpretation** of public information that others haven’t realized.

### 7. **Market Impact: Informed vs. Uninformed Flow**
- In **order-driven markets** (stocks), a large trade moves the price the same whether it’s informed or not.
- In **quote-driven markets** (sports betting), market makers adjust quotes when they detect informed flow.
- **Implication:** In equities, you can’t easily hide whether your trade is informed—size alone signals intent.

## 📌 Key Insights

### ✅ **Shift Your Mindset**
- Move from **“What do I know?”** to **“What does the market know?”**
- The market price is a **composite of all participants’ information**—respect it.

### 🧩 **Identify Your Edge**
- Ask: **“What do I know that the market doesn’t?”** and **“Why doesn’t the market know this?”**
- Avoid **generic valuation metrics** as a standalone thesis—they’re likely already priced in.

### 🔍 **Analyze Counterparty Behavior**
- Before trading, consider:
    1. **Who is short and why?**
    2. **Who looked but didn’t buy? Why?**
    3. **Who sold recently? Why?**
- This helps contextualize your conviction and anticipate price movements.

### 📉 **Trade with Market Awareness**
- In **small/mid caps**, flow analysis is especially valuable—you can often infer who is trading.
- **Index rebalancing, retail sentiment, insider filings, and quant flows** leave detectable footprints.

### ⚠️ **Avoid Common Pitfalls**
- Don’t assume you’re the **only one with insight**.
- Don’t trade solely on **publicly available data** without a unique angle.
- Don’t ignore **market signals** that contradict your private information.

### 🧪 **Simulation as a Learning Tool**
- The **Rotman Interactive Trader (RIT)** platform allows practice in a controlled, repeatable environment.
- **Repetition and review** build intuition for price discovery dynamics.
